When the PHP community was active in the past, many frameworks sprang up like mushrooms after rain, and many distinctive frameworks gave people a bright impression. However, when I recently revisited those framework repos, they were either archived or almost not maintained. In terms of resident process/coroutine/asynchronous solutions, the division between domestic and foreign countries is relatively obvious. Domestically, swoole/workerman solutions are preferred, while foreign countries prefer reactphp based on fibers. Swoole uses a hook solution, and the entire asynchronous/coroutine experience is relatively smooth. When fibers were proposed, it was highly anticipated, and the entire PHP community hoped to unify and prosper the PHP coroutine/asynchronous IO ecosystem. But after so many years, it seems that things have gone against expectations. As a third-party extension, Swoole's library authors don't consider adapting it specifically. PHPers can only wait for the official to pay attention to coroutine/asynchronous support again. I don't know how long we have to wait this time. PHP itself also has some problems that need to be solved urgently: php-fpm is a big problem. When a company is very small, fpm is indeed effective and solves (hides) many problems, but once the business scale rises to a certain level, it becomes a burden. Every request has to go through the process of initializing the framework again, which brings a lot of unnecessary performance overhead. As a scripting language, PHP is not weak in performance. Fpm is like a shackle, hindering the release of PHP's performance, which has become a very important reason for large companies to abandon PHP. Of course, you say that we have reactphp/workerman/swoole resident process solutions, but for the same reason, without official support for the server under cli, small groups fight on their own, and exert their strength in different directions, and can't make much waves. Type problem: 1. PHP succeeds with array, and fails with array. There is a reason for this sentence. Associative arrays are too easy to use. No matter what you do, the first thing you think of is to use array to hold it, and as long as there is one person in the group, associative arrays will fly around, which will greatly reduce the readability and maintainability of the project. Therefore, in my project, associative arrays are only allowed to be used inside methods, and an associative array cannot be passed outside the method. If you want to, please convert it into an object. Even so, you can only restrain yourself, and you cannot restrain third-party SDKs or open source libraries, and it is difficult to completely prevent the abuse of associative arrays. 2. Lack of generic support. Of course, weakly typed languages are not so eager for generic support, but the deeper a PHP project is, the more it feels that there should be generics. Of course, phpdoc helps us declare generics to get IDE support, You have to write phpdoc when you write code, which makes the whole development experience very fragmented and the experience is greatly reduced. Other advanced developments use multi-process/thread/coroutine/lock/timer, etc., not to mention the mess, and the peripheral ecosystem does not have an official language server implementation, so developers can only rely on paid plugins/phpstorm to get a good development experience. The complex environment setup makes the cost of getting started with the language high (there seem to be some community solutions, but they can only be used for development, not production-level). PHP planning issues: The views here are relatively subjective. I think the current PHP community lacks an authoritative leader to lead the planning of PHP development, who has the courage and strength to lead PHP out of its current predicament. As far as I know, the PHP Foundation currently has a total of 10 full-time and part-time developers. Swoole also has many developers in the PHP development team. The development power is not weak, but in recent years, PHP's updates have not been surprising.
